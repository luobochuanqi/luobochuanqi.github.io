London’s metro, launched in 1863, is the oldest and longest of all six, with a route of 394 kilometres. Paris follows at 199 kilometres — almost half London’s total — and records a higher annual passenger volume. Tokyo ranks third in route length yet leads all cities in ridership, transporting 1.927 billion passengers per year.
By contrast, the three modern metro systems are considerably smaller. Among these newer networks, Washington D.C.’s is the largest, stretching 126 kilometres. ## 议论文

- 开头:
  1. the sight of ... has become routine.

- Body:
  1. The first problem
  2. Equally concerning
  3. Finally, and most overlooked

- 结尾:
  1. In short,

```
Title: The Hidden Costs of Food Delivery on Campus
In almost every Chinese university dormitory, the sight of a delivery rider handing over a steaming bag has become routine. Apps like Meituan and Ele.me offer undeniable convenience, yet this habit comes with serious consequences. Despite saving time, food delivery hurts Chinese college students' health, wallets, and social connections.
The first problem is physical health. Most delivered meals from popular restaurants are loaded with oil, salt, and MSG to enhance flavor. A student at Nanjing University who orders twice a day may consume double the recommended sodium intake. Over a semester, this leads to weight gain, skin problems, and chronic fatigue. Unlike university canteens, which have basic nutrition standards, delivery apps encourage greasy, heavy dishes that lack fresh vegetables. Some students even develop digestive issues after months of relying on takeout.
Equally concerning is the financial strain. A typical meal on Ele.me costs 20-25 yuan including delivery fees. Ordering five times a week adds up to 100-125 yuan, while a canteen meal is only 10-15 yuan. For a student from a low-income family in rural Gansu, that extra 50-75 yuan per week could pay for textbooks or daily necessities. Many students unconsciously blow through their monthly allowance because delivery's "small" individual charges pile up quickly. What starts as a convenience ends up as a serious budget problem.
Finally, and most overlooked, is the loss of social interaction. Eating alone in a dorm room while watching short videos replaces the lively canteen experience where friends chat, share dishes, and bond. At Zhejiang University, canteens are not just for eating — they are where students form study groups, celebrate birthdays, and relax after exams. When delivery becomes the default, these spontaneous interactions disappear. First-year students, far from home, may feel especially isolated without the daily ritual of sharing meals with peers.
In short, the convenience of food delivery hides real costs: poor health, wasted money, and weakened friendships. Chinese students would be better off walking to the canteen or cooking simple meals with roommates. The short-term ease is not worth the long-term price.

标题：校园外卖背后的隐性代价
在中国几乎所有大学宿舍楼下，外卖骑手递出热气腾腾餐袋的场景早已司空见惯。美团、饿了么等软件固然带来无可否认的便利，但这种消费习惯暗藏诸多隐患。外卖看似省时，实则损害大学生的身体健康、加重经济负担，还会割裂人际社交。

首要问题是身体健康受损。市面上大多数外卖为追求口感，重油、重盐、多放味精。南京大学一名学生如果一天点两次外卖，摄入的钠含量会达到推荐标准的两倍。一学期下来，容易发胖、皮肤变差，还会长期疲惫乏力。学校食堂有基础营养管控标准，而外卖平台主推油腻厚重、缺少新鲜蔬菜的菜品。不少学生长期吃外卖数月后，出现肠胃不适等病症。

同样不容忽视的是经济压力。饿了么一份普通餐食含配送费需要 20 至 25 元，一周点五次就要花费 100 至 125 元；而食堂一餐仅需 10 至 15 元。对于来自甘肃农村低收入家庭的学生，每周多花的 50 至 75 元本可以用来购买教材和生活用品。单次外卖开销看似不多，但日积月累，很多学生不知不觉就花光了每月生活费。最初贪图便利，最后演变成严重的收支危机。

最后一点，也是最容易被忽视的，是社交缺失。独自待在宿舍一边刷短视频一边吃饭，取代了在食堂和好友聊天、分餐、增进感情的热闹时光。在浙江大学，食堂不只是吃饭的地方，更是学生组建学习小组、庆祝生日、考完试放松闲聊的场所。当点外卖成为常态，这些自然而然的线下交流便消失了。远离家乡的大一新生，失去和同学结伴吃饭的日常，孤独感会愈发强烈。

简言之，外卖的便利之下藏着实实在在的代价：健康变差、钱财浪费、友情变淡。大学生最好多去食堂就餐，或是和室友一起简单做饭。短暂的舒适，不值得付出长远的代价。
```
